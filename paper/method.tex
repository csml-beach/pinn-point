\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

We study how the choice of interior collocation points affects the accuracy and
convergence of physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) trained on geometrically nontrivial
PDEs.  Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the physical domain,
with boundary $\partial\Omega$, and let $u:\Omega\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^m$ be the
solution field.  We consider the PDE problem
of the form
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{N}[u](x) = f(x),
    \quad x\in\Omega,
    \qquad
    \mathcal{B}[u](x) = g(x),
    \quad x\in\partial\Omega,
    \label{eq:pde-general}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}$ is the differential PDE operator, $\mathcal{B}$ is the boundary
operator, and $f$ and $g$ are the forcing and boundary terms.
For time-dependent problems, time is treated as an additional input coordinate
and the space-time domain is sampled by the same collocation interface.

\subsection{PINN Objective}
\label{subsec:pinn-objective}

Following the standard PINN formulation \cite{raissi2019physics}, we train a
differentiable parametric model $u_\theta$ with parameters $\theta$ to
approximate the unknown solution field.  The defining feature of a PINN is the
use of automatic differentiation to compute the derivatives required by the
PDE and boundary conditions.  The model is trained by minimizing a composite
loss combining supervised data observations, the interior PDE residual, and
boundary terms:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta)
    =
    w_d \mathcal{L}_d(\theta;\mathcal{D})
    + w_r \mathcal{L}_r(\theta;\mathcal{X}_r)
    + w_b \mathcal{L}_b(\theta;\mathcal{X}_b).
    \label{eq:pinn-objective}
\end{equation}
Here $\mathcal{D}$ is a supervised dataset of solution observations, which may
be exact or noisy, $\mathcal{X}_r\subset\Omega$ is the interior collocation set, and
$\mathcal{X}_b\subset\partial\Omega$ is the set used for boundary enforcement.
The interior residual loss evaluates
the PDE constraint violation across the collocation set:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_r(\theta;\mathcal{X}_r)
    =
    \frac{1}{|\mathcal{X}_r|}
    \sum_{x\in\mathcal{X}_r}
    \left\|
        \mathcal{R}_\theta(x)
    \right\|_2^2,
    \qquad
    \mathcal{R}_\theta (x)
    =
    \mathcal{N}[u_\theta](x)-f(x).
    \label{eq:residual-loss}
\end{equation}
The central methodological question is how to choose $\mathcal{X}_r$. An adaptive collocation-point sampler needs a scalar field that identifies where the current PINN iterate violates the governing equation based on the current collocation set.  We next describe the residual-scoring scaffold, before proposing the new adaptive interior collocation rule.
\subsection{Mesh-Backed Residual Scoring}
\label{subsec:mesh-backed-adaptive}

  We use a finite-element mesh as a geometric scaffold for localizing residual information.  This mesh provides a structured way to evaluate the residual at specific points and assign scores to each element based on the residual magnitude.  The scores are then used to rank the elements and define an spatial importance measure, which is subsequently sampled to determine the next collocation points.  This allows the sampler to identify regions in the computational domain where the current PINN model deviates most from the governing equations, and add collocation points would be most beneficial for improving the approximation.

For a PINN iterate $u_{\theta_k}$, the strong-form residual is
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{s}}_{\theta_k}(x)
    :=
    \mathcal{N}[u_{\theta_k}](x)-f(x),
    \qquad x\in\Omega .
    \label{eq:strong-residual}
\end{equation}
To localize this residual, let
$\mathcal{T}_h^{(k)}=\{E_i^{(k)}\}_{i=1}^{M_k}$ be an evolving finite-element mesh
on $\Omega$ at iteration $k$.  The mesh partitions the domain into
elements on which residual energy can be estimated and compared.

We next compute a quadrature approximation
to the residual energy.  Let
$Q_i^{(k)}=\{q_{iq}\}_{q=1}^{n_q}$ be barycentric quadrature points on
$E_i^{(k)}$ with nonnegative weights $\{\omega_q\}_{q=1}^{n_q}$ satisfying
$\sum_q\omega_q=1$.  We assign element $E_i^{(k)}$ the score
\begin{equation}
    s_i^{(k)}
    =
    |E_i^{(k)}|^{\chi}
    \sum_{q=1}^{n_q}
    \omega_q
    \left\|
        \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{s}}_{\theta_k}(q_{iq})
    \right\|_2^2,
    \label{eq:element-indicator}
\end{equation}
where $|E_i^{(k)}|$ is the element area or volume and $\chi$ controls how much
geometric measure affects the sampling score.  When $\chi=1$, this expression is
a quadrature approximation to the elemental integral
$\int_{E_i^{(k)}}\|\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{s}}_{\theta_k}(x)\|_2^2\,\dd x$. Lower
values of $\chi$ shift the score toward residual magnitude.

\begin{remark}[Stabilization of the residual measure]
The score $s_i^{(k)}$ is a mesh-local residual-energy indicator, not a
Galerkin projection of the residual.  Its purpose is to define a discrete
importance measure on the current mesh.  In computation, however, this measure
is estimated from a partially trained neural network and can be affected by
optimizer transients, finite quadrature, and localized residual spikes.  Before
using the scores for point allocation, we therefore apply three stabilizing
steps: local graph smoothing, rank normalization, and cross-iteration
persistence.

The local smoothing step blends each score with the average of
its neighboring element scores,
\begin{equation}
    \tilde{s}_i^{(k)}
    =
    (1-\lambda_s)s_i^{(k)}
    +
    \lambda_s
    \frac{\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)} |E_j^{(k)}|s_j^{(k)}}
         {\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}(i)} |E_j^{(k)}|},
    \label{eq:score-smoothing}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}(i)$ is the set of neighboring elements and
$\lambda_s\in[0,1]$ is a smoothing parameter.  This step suppresses isolated
elementwise fluctuations while preserving coherent high-residual regions.  The
smoothed scores are then converted into normalized ranks
\begin{equation}
    z_i^{(k)}
    =
    \frac{
        \operatorname{rank}\!\left(\tilde{s}_i^{(k)}\right)-1
    }{
        M_k-1
    },
    \qquad
    z_i^{(k)}\in[0,1],
    \label{eq:normalized-rank}
\end{equation}
where rank one corresponds to the smallest smoothed score and rank $M_k$ to the
largest.  This ensures that the allocation depends on the ordering of
elementwise residual indicators rather than their absolute scale.  Finally,
cross-iteration smoothness is introduced by blending the current rank with the
stabilized score from the previous iteration:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{z}_i^{(k)}
    =
    \alpha \hat{z}_{\pi(i)}^{(k-1)}
    +
    (1-\alpha) z_i^{(k)},
    \label{eq:rank-persistent-score}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha\in[0,1]$ is a persistence coefficient and $\pi(i)$ maps element
$E_i^{(k)}$ to the nearest element in the previous mesh iteration.  During
the warmup stage, when no previous scaffold is available, we set
$\hat{z}_i^{(k)}=z_i^{(k)}$.  The stabilized quantities
$\hat{z}_i^{(k)}$ are the residual scores used by the collocation-point sampler
below.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Power-Tempered Collocation-Point Sampler}
\label{subsec:power-tempered}

We follow the general principle of residual-adaptive
collocation \cite{wu2023comprehensive} where new points are allocated in regions where the current PINN residual is large. However, in contrast to
pointwise residual-density methods, we define the point distribution over
mesh elements and allocate a
fixed-budget based on the stabilized element scores
$\hat{z}_i^{(k)}$.

We begin with a geometric coverage weight $|E_i^{(k)}|^\gamma$
and tilt it by the stabilized residual rank through an exponential factor:

\begin{equation}
    p_i^{(k)}
    =
    \frac{
        |E_i^{(k)}|^\gamma
        \exp\!\left(\beta_k \hat{z}_i^{(k)}\right)
    }{
        \sum_{j=1}^{M_k}
        |E_j^{(k)}|^\gamma
        \exp\!\left(\beta_k \hat{z}_j^{(k)}\right)
    },
    \label{eq:apt-distribution}
\end{equation}
The exponent $\gamma$ controls the contribution of element measure: larger
values move the distribution toward area- or volume-proportional coverage,
whereas smaller values give relatively more influence to residual rank.  The
temperature $\beta_k$ controls how sharply the distribution favors the largest
persistent ranks.  Rather than fixing this sharpness a priori, we relate it to
the concentration of the residual field.  Let
\begin{equation}
    H_k
    =
    -\frac{1}{\log M_k}
    \sum_{i=1}^{M_k}
    \rho_i^{(k)}\log\rho_i^{(k)},
    \qquad
    \rho_i^{(k)}
    =
    \frac{\tilde{s}_i^{(k)}}{\sum_j\tilde{s}_j^{(k)}}.
    \label{eq:residual-entropy}
\end{equation}
be the normalized entropy of the smoothed residual scores.  We set
\begin{equation}
    \beta_k
    =
    \beta_{\min}
    +
    \left(\beta_{\max}-\beta_{\min}\right)(1-H_k).
    \label{eq:apt-temperature}
\end{equation}
When the residual scores are diffuse, $H_k$ is close to one, $\beta_k$ is near
$\beta_{\min}$, and the allocation remains close to the geometric coverage
weight.  When the residual scores are concentrated, $H_k$ decreases and the
temperature moves toward $\beta_{\max}$, increasing the allocation to
persistent high-residual regions.  This entropy control prevents the method
from applying an overly sharp residual bias when the residual field does not
yet contain a coherent localization signal.

Given probabilities $p_i^{(k)}$ and residual-point budget $N_r$, integer
element quotas are assigned deterministically by largest remainder:
\begin{equation}
    n_i^{(k)}
    =
    \left\lfloor N_r p_i^{(k)} \right\rfloor
    +
    \delta_i,
    \qquad
    \sum_i n_i^{(k)} = N_r,
    \label{eq:largest-remainder}
\end{equation}
where the $\delta_i$ allocate the remaining points to the elements with the
largest fractional remainders.  This deterministic rounding preserves the fixed
collocation budget while avoiding additional multinomial noise in the number of
points assigned to each element.  The next residual collocation set is then
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{X}_r^{(k+1)}
    =
    \bigcup_{i=1}^{M_k}
    \left\{
        x_{i\ell}^{(k+1)}
    \right\}_{\ell=1}^{n_i^{(k)}},
    \qquad
    x_{i\ell}^{(k+1)} \sim \operatorname{Unif}\!\left(E_i^{(k)}\right).
    \label{eq:collocation-update}
\end{equation}
In practice, the uniform samples inside each selected triangle or tetrahedron
are generated with barycentric coordinates.  Thus the current network state
$u_{\theta_k}$ determines a mesh-local residual measure, this measure induces
element probabilities, and those probabilities define the collocation points
used in the next training stage.

\subsection{Comparison Samplers}
\label{subsec:comparison-samplers}

We compare the power-tempered adaptive method against fixed, pointwise
adaptive, and scaffold-based alternatives.  Uniform random sampling draws
interior points by rejection sampling from the domain bounding box.  Halton
sampling uses a low-discrepancy sequence with the same rejection interface and
advances to a fresh, non-overlapping sequence window at each iteration.  Plain
Halton is therefore a non-adaptive baseline and does not use the mesh-backed
residual scores.  RAD follows residual-based adaptive distribution sampling:
\begin{equation}
    \Pr(x_j)
    =
    \frac{
        |\mathcal{R}_{\theta}(x_j)|^\kappa/
        \overline{|\mathcal{R}_{\theta}|^\kappa}
        + c
    }{
        \sum_{\ell}
        \left(
        |\mathcal{R}_{\theta}(x_\ell)|^\kappa/
        \overline{|\mathcal{R}_{\theta}|^\kappa}
        + c
        \right)
    },
    \label{eq:rad-weighting}
\end{equation}
where $\{x_j\}$ is a candidate set, $\kappa$ is the residual exponent,
and $c$ is a coverage regularizer.  In contrast to the mesh-scaffold method,
RAD samples from pointwise residual weights on a candidate cloud rather than
from element-level residual scores.

We also evaluate a Halton-backed adaptive variant as a scaffold-based
comparison.  This method uses the same mesh-local residual scores and
cross-iteration rank persistence as the proposed method, but reserves a fixed
fraction of the residual-point budget for a Halton coverage backbone and
allocates only the remaining points adaptively.  It therefore tests whether the
power-tempered element allocation in \cref{subsec:power-tempered} improves on a
simpler combination of residual scoring and low-discrepancy coverage.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{subsec:evaluation-metrics}

For each method and seed, the reported checkpoint is selected by the validation
score recorded during training.  We evaluate solution error against the FEM
reference solution on a fixed reference mesh:
\begin{equation}
    e_{\mathrm{rel}}
    =
    \frac{
        \left\|u_\theta-u_{\mathrm{ref}}\right\|_{L^2(\Omega)}
    }{
        \left\|u_{\mathrm{ref}}\right\|_{L^2(\Omega)}
    },
    \qquad
    r_{\mathrm{fixed}}
    =
    \left(
        \frac{1}{|\Omega|}
        \int_{\Omega}
        \left\|\mathcal{R}_{\theta}(x)\right\|_2^2
        \dd x
    \right)^{1/2}.
    \label{eq:evaluation-metrics}
\end{equation}
The fixed residual metric is computed on the same reference discretization for
all methods in a run.  Runtime is recorded per iteration and accumulated through
the selected checkpoint so that sampler overhead is part of the comparison.
