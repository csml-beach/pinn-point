\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

We consider the problem of finding an approximate solution $u_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ to a partial differential equation (PDE) defined on a bounded domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ with a complex, potentially multi-connected boundary $\partial \Omega$. The governing equations are given by:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pde_general}
    \mathcal{F}[u](\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{x}), \quad \mathbf{x} \in \Omega
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathcal{F}[\cdot]$ denotes a (possibly nonlinear) differential operator, $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is a given forcing term, and $\mathcal{B}[\cdot]$ encodes the boundary conditions.
subject to the boundary condition $\mathcal{B}[u](\mathbf{x}) = g(\mathbf{x})$ on $\partial \Omega$. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) approximate $u(\mathbf{x})$ using a neural network $u_\theta$ with trainable parameters $\theta$. The network is optimized by minimizing a composite objective function:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \lambda_r \mathcal{L}_r(\theta; \mathcal{X}_r) + \lambda_b \mathcal{L}_b(\theta; \mathcal{X}_b) + \lambda_d \mathcal{L}_d(\theta; \mathcal{X}_d)
\end{equation}
Here $\mathcal{L}_r$, $\mathcal{L}_b$, and $\mathcal{L}_d$ are the interior residual, boundary condition, and supervised data losses, respectively; $\lambda_r, \lambda_b, \lambda_d \geq 0$ are scalar weights balancing their contributions; and $\mathcal{X}_r \subset \Omega$, $\mathcal{X}_b \subset \partial\Omega$, $\mathcal{X}_d$ are the corresponding point sets. 

The residual loss is defined as the mean squared PDE residual over the interior collocation set:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_r(\theta; \mathcal{X}_r) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{X}_r|} \sum_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}_r} \|\mathcal{R}[u_\theta](\mathbf{x})\|^2, \quad \mathcal{R}[u_\theta] \equiv \mathcal{F}[u_\theta] - f
\end{equation}
The accuracy of the PINN approximation is critically sensitive to the distribution of $\mathcal{X}_r$, particularly in regions where the solution $u$ exhibits sharp gradients or the domain $\Omega$ possesses complex geometric features.

\subsection{The Mesh-Guided Computational Scaffold}
We propose a hybrid framework that couples simplicial mesh evolution ($h$-refinement) with adaptive importance sampling. The domain $\Omega$ is discretized by a sequence of conforming triangular meshes $\{\mathcal{M}^{(k)}\}_{k=0}^K$, where each mesh $\mathcal{M} = \{E_i\}_{i=1}^{N_e}$ consists of $N_e$ elements such that $\overline{\Omega} = \cup_{i=1}^{N_e} E_i$ and the intersection of any two elements is either empty, a common vertex, or a common edge.

\subsubsection{H-Refinement and Local Error Indicators}
For each element $E_i \in \mathcal{M}^{(k)}$, we define a local error indicator $\eta_i^{(k)}$ based on the $L^2$-norm of the current PDE residual:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_i^{(k)} = \left( \int_{E_i} \|\mathcal{R}[u_\theta](\mathbf{x})\|^2 d\mathbf{x} \right)^{1/2}
\end{equation}
In our implementation, this integral is approximated using a high-order vertex-averaging scheme on the mesh. Elements are marked for refinement if they exceed a relative threshold: $\eta_i > \tau \cdot \max_{j} \eta_j$, where $\tau \in (0, 1]$ is the refinement aggressiveness. The mesh is updated via a refinement operator $\mathcal{T}: \mathcal{M}^{(k)} \to \mathcal{M}^{(k+1)}$ (e.g., longest-edge bisection), ensuring that the scaffold density $\rho(\mathbf{x})$ tracks the localized residual field.

\subsubsection{Rank-Persistent Field Projection}
To stabilize the sampling process against transient optimizer oscillations, we compute a rank-persistent score $\hat{z}_i^{(k)}$. We first map the indicator $\eta_i^{(k)}$ to its normalized ordinal rank:
\begin{equation}
    z_i^{(k)} = \frac{\sigma(\eta_i^{(k)}) - 1}{N_e - 1}, \quad \sigma(\eta) \equiv \text{rank of } \eta \text{ in } \{\eta_j\}_{j=1}^{N_e}
\end{equation}
The persistent score $\hat{z}^{(k)}$ is then defined as an exponentially weighted moving average:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{z}_i^{(k)} = \alpha \hat{z}_{\pi(i)}^{(k-1)} + (1-\alpha) z_i^{(k)}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ is the persistence factor and $\pi: \mathcal{M}^{(k)} \to \mathcal{M}^{(k-1)}$ is a spatial projection operator that maps each element in the refined mesh to its parent or nearest neighbor in the previous discretization.

\subsection{Adaptive Importance Sampling Densities}
The mesh scaffold $\mathcal{M}^{(k)}$ induces a piecewise constant probability density function (PDF) $f_{\mathcal{X}}^{(k)}(\mathbf{x})$ over the domain $\Omega$:
\begin{equation}
    f_{\mathcal{X}}^{(k)}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_e} \frac{P(E_i)}{\text{Area}(E_i)} \mathbb{I}_{E_i}(\mathbf{x})
\end{equation}
where $\mathbb{I}_{E_i}$ is the indicator function for element $E_i$, and $P(E_i)$ is the discrete probability mass assigned to that element. We propose two strategies for defining $P(E_i)$, both governed by an information-theoretic control loop.

Let $\mathcal{H}_{\text{norm}} = -(1/\ln N_e) \sum p_i \ln p_i$ be the normalized entropy of the residual distribution $p_i = \eta_i / \sum \eta_j$. We define the residual concentration $C = 1 - \mathcal{H}_{\text{norm}}$ as the driver for adaptivity.

\subsubsection{Power-Tempered Sampling}
The Power-Tempered strategy employs exponential tilting of the geometric baseline to focus the collocation set:
\begin{equation}
    P(E_i) = \frac{\text{Area}_i^{\gamma} \exp\left( \beta \hat{z}_i \right)}{\sum_{j=1}^{N_e} \text{Area}_j^{\gamma} \exp\left( \beta \hat{z}_j \right)}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ is a geometric scaling exponent. The inverse temperature $\beta$ is autonomously adjusted:
\begin{equation}
    \beta(C) = \beta_{\min} + (\beta_{\max} - \beta_{\min}) \cdot C
\end{equation}
This formulation ensures that the sampling density $f_{\mathcal{X}}$ smoothly transitions from a nearly uniform geometric distribution (high entropy, $C \to 0$) to a sharply peaked distribution targeting high-residual corridors (low entropy, $C \to 1$).

\subsubsection{Entropy-Balanced Sampling}
The Entropy-Balanced strategy uses a dynamic convex combination of geometric and adaptive measures:
\begin{equation}
    P(E_i) = (1 - \lambda) \frac{\text{Area}_i}{\text{Area}(\Omega)} + \lambda \frac{\hat{z}_i^\kappa}{\sum \hat{z}_j^\kappa}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$ is a rank-sensitivity parameter. The mixing coefficient $\lambda(C) = \lambda_{\min} + (\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min}) C$ ensures a robust coverage floor, preventing the "collapse" of the collocation set into a few mesh elements and maintaining physical constraints in low-residual regions.

\subsection{Deterministic Allocation via the Largest Remainder Method}
Given a global budget of $N_r$ collocation points, we must distribute them into integer quotas $n_i$ per element. This is framed as a constrained optimization problem:
\begin{equation}
    \text{minimize} \sum_{i=1}^{N_e} |n_i - N_r P(E_i)| \quad \text{s.t.} \sum_{i=1}^{N_e} n_i = N_r, \ n_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}
\end{equation}
This is solved exactly using the Largest Remainder Method, where initial quotas are set to $\lfloor N_r P(E_i) \rfloor$ and the residual points are allocated to elements with the largest fractional parts. Points within each triangle are then sampled via an affine transformation from the reference unit simplex $\hat{E} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ to $E_i$.
